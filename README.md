      <details open>
        <summary>Let me tell you a story.</summary>

        <p>
          I’m an immigrant founder from Tajikistan. Growing up, my life split into two obsessions: sport and science.
        </p>

        <p>
          My first “research project” was at 13—playing with simulations inspired by Einstein’s general relativity, trying to estimate the age of the universe by changing assumptions about matter and energy.
          At 19, I built signal-processing algorithms for LiDAR—my first real taste of turning physics into something that works in the real world.
        </p>

        <p>
          Sport was my other teacher. I trained across sambo/judo/freestyle wrestling at university, later got addicted to surfing, and along the way I learned a simple truth:
          progress comes from feedback loops—practice, error, correction, repeat.
        </p>

        <p>
          Academically, I kept jumping across worlds because I didn’t want to only think about reality—I wanted to touch it.
          I moved from astrophysics (with Princeton-linked collaborations) to quantum optics (Oxford-linked work), then into robotics and AI:
          computer vision, 3D reconstruction, and reinforcement learning for embodied systems.
        </p>

        <p>
          Two years of doctoral research with Prof. Andrzej Cichocki — BCI, cognitive simulations, and tensor networks; adjacent work touching ICA and matrix/tensor factorization.
        </p>

        <p>
          My first startup started the simplest way: build, ship, repeat. We created 200 web-based educational simulations used by 500,000+ students.
          I sold that company at 23 in a seven-figure exit to one of the largest banks in Central Europe.
        </p>

        <p>
          Then I watched <em>Ready Player One</em> and couldn’t unsee the future: education should feel like a game you can’t stop playing.
          That became XReadyLab—interactive STEM simulations (web/VR) with AI tutors.
        </p>

        <p>
          Over time—through conversations and collaborators around MIT, Stanford brain research circles, and EPFL—it clicked:
          we weren’t just making content. We were building a Learning OS: a closed-loop system that improves through real-time feedback.
        </p>

        <p><strong>First for students. Next for robots.</strong></p>

        <p>
          Physical AI won’t get to human-level robustness by scraping half the internet or living only in simulations.
          It will get there by learning the way humans do—through experience, feedback, and expert intuition, grounded in the physics of the real world.
        </p>
      </details>
